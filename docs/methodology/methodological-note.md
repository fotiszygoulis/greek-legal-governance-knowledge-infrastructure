# Methodological Note

## From PDF to Linked Legal-Governance Knowledge

The analytical use of legal and administrative documents depends not only on their public availability, but also on their structural accessibility. In the Greek legal and governance context, a substantial portion of normative and administrative material remains available primarily in static PDF format. Although such documents are formally accessible, they are often difficult to reuse systematically for institutional analysis, legal mapping, regulatory traceability, or AI-assisted research workflows.

This methodological note outlines an exploratory workflow for transforming Greek legal and administrative documents from static PDF form into structured, interlinked, and machine-usable textual resources. The objective is not limited to text extraction. Rather, it concerns the construction of a legal-governance knowledge layer through which documents become more navigable, more comparable, and more analytically usable in governance-oriented research environments.

The workflow begins from publicly available legal and administrative documents, especially materials that are formally authoritative or operationally relevant to governance research. These may include statutes, decrees, ministerial decisions, implementing acts, administrative circulars, competence-defining texts, or institutional regulations.

The first technical stage involves parsing source PDFs into a cleaner and more tractable textual format, such as markdown. This conversion is methodologically significant because it creates the conditions for segmentation, linking, annotation, and downstream retrieval. Once converted into structured text, documents must be segmented into meaningful analytical units, such as articles, paragraphs, sections, competence clauses, procedural rules, definitional fragments, or institution-specific mandates.

A linked legal-governance corpus requires not only structured text but also relational visibility. Legal and administrative documents derive much of their meaning from references to other texts, institutions, procedures, and normative layers. Accordingly, the next methodological step consists in identifying and operationalizing linkages across documents.

This approach improves retrieval, navigation, and traceability in ways that are more compatible with governance-oriented legal analysis. At the same time, it must be treated with methodological caution. A linked corpus is not equivalent to legal interpretation. It can improve access, retrieval, and analytical organization, but it cannot resolve doctrinal ambiguity, institutional conflict, or jurisprudential meaning by itself.

Within AGRIR-Lab, this workflow should be treated as an emerging research infrastructure pathway. Its contribution lies in showing how legal and administrative texts can be reorganized into a more useful analytical substrate for the study of governance systems, institutional resilience, public-sector oversight, compliance architectures, and regulatory complexity.
